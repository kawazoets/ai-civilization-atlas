# When Compute Met Interest Rates

## Why I Started Thinking About Interest Rates Differently

I originally started studying Japanese real estate.

The topic was simple.

**Interest rates.**

At first, I thought this was merely a discussion about housing loans and property investment.

However, the deeper I explored the subject, the more I realized that interest rates are not just about real estate.

They determine the cost of capital.

That realization immediately connected to another question.

AI companies are investing enormous amounts of capital into Compute long before generating profits.

Traditional investment decisions are usually evaluated against interest rates.

If capital can earn a predictable return elsewhere, any investment must justify taking additional risk.

But Compute investment appears to operate differently.

Companies such as OpenAI and Anthropic continue to attract extraordinary amounts of private capital despite the absence of public-market liquidity.

This made me wonder whether Compute is competing with interest rates at all.

Or whether investors believe Compute belongs to an entirely different return category.

That question naturally led me to IPOs.

Perhaps IPOs are no longer the central mechanism for raising capital.

Instead, they may increasingly function as liquidity events for founders, employees, and early investors.

If that is true, another question immediately follows.

**Where does AI-created wealth go after liquidity?**

Does it return to the next generation of AI companies?

Does it move into real estate?

Does it become long-term capital preservation?

The discussion had started with Japanese interest rates.

Yet it unexpectedly expanded into Compute, IPOs, liquidity, and capital allocation.

This was the moment I realized that the real subject was no longer interest rates.

It was the movement of capital itself.

---

## Learning Note

Today I realized that collecting knowledge is no longer the difficult part.

AI can explain real estate, interest rates, IPOs, and private markets within hours.

The real challenge is connecting these subjects into a single structure.

Perhaps the most valuable research question is not:

**"What is interest?"**

but

**"How does capital move through the AI economy?"**
