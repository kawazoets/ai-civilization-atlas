Last Monday marked the beginning of the second week of July.

It was supposed to be the day when new AMRs manufactured in China would arrive at our FC-BGA substrate manufacturing site.

Everyone had been preparing for the installation.

Instead, we received different news.

The shipment had been delayed.

A typhoon had disrupted the shipping schedule, and the vessel carrying the equipment could not arrive as planned.

As a result, not only the installation schedule but also parts of the factory's overall construction timeline had to be adjusted.

For most people, this might seem like an ordinary logistics issue.

For me, however, it changed the way I think about AI infrastructure.

When people talk about AI, the discussion usually focuses on software.

New models.

GPUs.

Inference speed.

Reasoning capability.

Yet none of those things mattered that morning.

The AI-related equipment we were waiting for was not delayed because of algorithms.

It was delayed because a ship could not arrive.

That simple fact reminded me that AI infrastructure still depends on the physical world.

Before equipment begins operating inside a factory, it must first be manufactured.

Then it must be loaded onto a ship.

It crosses national borders.

It arrives at a port.

It passes customs.

It is transported to the factory.

Only after installation, wiring, testing, and commissioning does it become part of an AI manufacturing system.

Watching that process from inside the project made me realize how much of AI exists long before software ever begins running.

The digital world often feels instantaneous.

Models can be downloaded in minutes.

Cloud services can be deployed almost immediately.

Software updates happen overnight.

Physical infrastructure operates differently.

Factories cannot be downloaded.

Production equipment cannot be copied.

A conveyor system cannot be installed through an internet connection.

An AMR still has to travel across the sea.

That distinction has become increasingly important to me.

Over the past several months, my understanding of AI has gradually shifted.

I first focused on GPUs.

Then I began observing FC-BGA substrate manufacturing.

Later I started studying factory control systems, electrical infrastructure, transformers, and industrial ecosystems.

Now another layer has appeared.

Logistics.

Without logistics, none of those physical systems can move.

The incident also reminded me of something else.

People often describe AI as if it were entirely digital.

In reality, AI depends on factories, ports, ships, warehouses, construction projects, electrical infrastructure, and thousands of people coordinating work across different countries.

The software may operate at the speed of computation.

The infrastructure still moves at the speed of the real world.

This experience did not teach me anything new about machine learning.

It taught me something about reality.

AI may be transforming the digital world.

But the infrastructure supporting AI still waits for ships.

*Learning Notes
AI infrastructure depends on physical logistics as much as digital technology.

Supply chains remain an essential part of AI infrastructure.
Software may scale instantly, but physical infrastructure develops at the pace of manufacturing and logistics.
Observing an actual project revealed a layer of AI that is often invisible in discussions focused only on models and computing power.
