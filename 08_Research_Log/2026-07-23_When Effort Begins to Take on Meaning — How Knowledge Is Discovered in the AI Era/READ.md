# When Effort Begins to Take on Meaning — How Knowledge Is Discovered in the AI Era

Over the past year, I have been writing research papers, publishing them with DOIs on Zenodo, documenting my Research Logs on GitHub, and sharing English articles on LinkedIn. To date, I have written more than 20 AI-related papers, over 40 records in both English and Japanese on GitHub, and have been publishing on LinkedIn almost every day.

To be honest, I originally thought these were all independent activities.

Zenodo was a place to publish papers.  
GitHub was a place to record research notes.  
LinkedIn was for professionals.  
Google Scholar was an academic search engine.  
Amazon was for selling books.

I believed these were completely separate worlds. Especially in the AI era, where traditional SEO no longer seems as decisive as it once was.

However, over the past few weeks, I have begun to see a different picture.

My paper *AI-Scored Society* was indexed by Google Scholar, followed by *AI Value Index (AIV)*.

Meanwhile, *AI Capitalism* has not yet appeared on Google Scholar. Nevertheless, on Zenodo it has by far the highest number of views and downloads among all of my papers.

Furthermore, although *AI × Gold* has not yet been indexed by Google Scholar, it has already been featured by Synapse, an AI-powered academic discovery and recommendation platform.

Even more interestingly, the English Kindle edition of *AI-Scored Society* appears not only on Amazon Japan but also in search results on Amazon India.

At first, I assumed these were simply unrelated coincidences.

But after looking at them together, I realized they were not.

The same research was being discovered through entirely different pathways.

Until recently, I believed that writing a paper was the research itself.

Lately, however, my perspective has begun to change.

Research does not end the moment it is published.

What happens afterward—how it is discovered, where it is recognized, and how it eventually reaches people—has become equally fascinating to observe.

The process itself has become worthy of observation, especially in an era where AI continuously reshapes how knowledge is distributed and updated.

Google Scholar serves as an entry point for academic discovery.

Zenodo serves as an entry point for open research publication.

Synapse serves as an AI-driven gateway for discovering research.

LinkedIn connects knowledge with practitioners.

Amazon provides another route through which knowledge circulates in the form of books.

Although these platforms appear to exist independently, they are gradually becoming connected.

What fascinated me most was that this transformation did not happen all at once.

Everything started as isolated points.

Writing a paper.

Obtaining a DOI.

Publishing a Research Log on GitHub.

Sharing it on LinkedIn.

Being indexed by Google Scholar.

Being featured on Synapse.

Appearing in Amazon search results across different countries.

Each of these was once an independent event.

Yet over time, they slowly began to form a single line.

Recently, I have become increasingly interested not only in creating content but also in understanding how that content travels through the world.

Perhaps in the AI era, our subject of observation is no longer limited to creating knowledge itself.

The structure through which knowledge is discovered and distributed may become just as important.

What impressed me most throughout this experience was a simple realization.

Points are never lines from the beginning.

Meaning emerges later.

Only because we continue recording one small point after another do we eventually look back and discover that they have begun connecting.

I feel that what I am observing now is precisely that process—the moment when isolated points begin to turn into a line.
