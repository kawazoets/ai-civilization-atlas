# Sharing a Biryani Lunch at Work — What It Means to Work in a Multinational Team

Recently, a lunch party was held at our worksite. It was organized by the Indian workers who work alongside us.

They prepared a large amount of biryani and served it to around twenty people working at the site, including Japanese, Chinese, Korean, Singaporean, and other colleagues.

The authentic biryani prepared by the Indian workers was the best biryani I have tasted in more than ten years of living in Singapore.

However, the valuable experience I gained that day was not limited to the taste of the biryani.

## Singapore’s Worksites Are Built on Multinational Teams

I currently work at a site where AI semiconductor-related equipment is being installed. Every day, Japanese, Chinese, Indian, Korean, and Singaporean workers move busily around the site.

Their roles are different, and so are the systems under which they work. They do not belong to the same companies, nor do they hold the same types of work visas.

Even so, they work together toward the shared goal of completing a single piece of equipment. Before coming to Singapore, I had never worked in an environment like this.

## Understanding the System Is Also Part of Working Effectively

Living in Singapore, I often encounter situations that remind me how important it is to understand not only the work itself, but also the systems governing it.

Recently, there was an incident involving the entry of a Japanese engineer into Singapore that made me think carefully about this.

The engineer was working in Singapore on a short-term basis. His work pass was allowed for up to ninety days, but after sixty days, he was informed by the ICA, Singapore’s immigration authority, that the pass could no longer be extended and that he had to leave Singapore.

It would have been better for him to leave quietly at that point. However, he submitted repeated online appeals requesting permission to remain for the full ninety days. In the end, he returned to Japan. By the time I advised him, the appeals had already been submitted.

One month later, when he attempted to enter Singapore again, he was denied entry immediately after getting off the plane and scanning his passport at the immigration machine. The matter had probably already been recorded in the system.

In Singapore, I often feel that rules and procedures are enforced strictly through administrative systems.

In Japan, people may sometimes think, “Perhaps they will understand if I explain it one more time.” In Singapore, however, there are many situations in which people are expected first to accept the system itself and then decide how to act within it.

I have experienced this difference many times during my ten years of living here.

Living with people from different cultures means understanding not only their language, but also the systems and ways of thinking of the country in which you live.

## Beyond the Work Permit System

Many of the Indian workers who served us biryani are staying in Singapore under Work Permits.

Many have left their families in their home countries and work here for a limited period. Their living conditions cannot necessarily be described as easy.

Even so, they have come to a foreign country and are using their skills to support their families.

At the worksite, we earn more than they do.

Despite that, they arranged a large amount of biryani themselves and generously shared it with everyone around them. There was no sense of hierarchy around the table. Instead, there was a feeling that we were all colleagues working at the same site.

## AI Infrastructure Is Built by People

When people discuss AI, they often focus on AI software, GPUs, data centers, semiconductors, and other technologies.

However, when you work at an AI manufacturing site, you see many things that cannot be understood through technology alone.

AI equipment is built by people.

Different nationalities.

Different cultures.

Different systems.

Different languages.

Different ways of working.

While overcoming these differences, people work together to complete a single piece of equipment. The biryani lunch reminded me of this once again.

## Learning Note

When I first came to Singapore, I thought that working in a foreign country was mainly a question of whether I could speak English.

After living here for more than ten years, I realized that this was not enough.

What is truly required is the ability to understand the country’s systems, build trust with people from different cultural backgrounds, and cooperate toward a shared goal.

AI infrastructure cannot be built by AI alone.

Behind it are people with many different backgrounds.

The biryani lunch was an experience that reminded me once again of this simple truth.
