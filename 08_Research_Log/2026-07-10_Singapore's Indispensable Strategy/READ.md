# Singapore's Indispensable Strategy

Singapore is widely recognized as a global financial center. However, for decades, the government has consistently maintained manufacturing as one of the country's key economic pillars.

Even today, manufacturing accounts for approximately 20% of Singapore's GDP. Under the **Manufacturing 2030** initiative, the government aims to increase manufacturing value-added by 50% by 2030 compared with 2020 levels. In other words, Singapore is not reducing its manufacturing sector; it is strengthening high-value manufacturing.

In particular, the government has placed strategic emphasis on three industries:

- Semiconductors
- Pharmaceuticals and biopharmaceuticals
- Aircraft Maintenance, Repair and Overhaul (MRO)

For a long time, I regarded these industries as separate sectors, each supported by its own independent policies.

My perspective began to change after I started working at a Broadcom-related FC-BGA substrate manufacturing site and observing the semiconductor industry from the factory floor.

Inside the factory, I was exposed every day not only to manufacturing equipment but also to AMRs, conveyor systems, control panels, PLCs, electrical infrastructure, piping systems, and cleanrooms—all essential components required to manufacture semiconductors at scale.

After reading news about Applied Materials' expansion project, I began running around the area where UMC, Soitec, SSMC, Micron, Jabil, and many other companies are concentrated, observing the industrial park firsthand.

What I found was far more than a collection of factories.

Semiconductor manufacturers, wafer material suppliers, equipment manufacturers, logistics infrastructure, roads, electrical systems, and water treatment facilities had all been organized into a single industrial cluster.

Singapore produces approximately 10% of the world's semiconductors and around 20% of global semiconductor manufacturing equipment. The semiconductor industry contributes roughly 6% of the country's GDP and employs more than 35,000 people.

For a country with such limited land and population, this represents an extraordinary industrial presence.

While researching the semiconductor industry, however, I realized that the same structural pattern existed elsewhere.

One example is the pharmaceutical and biopharmaceutical industry.

Singapore is home to manufacturing and research facilities operated by many of the world's leading pharmaceutical companies, including Pfizer, GSK, MSD, Sanofi, Amgen, AbbVie, Roche, and Novartis.

Today, eight of the world's top ten biopharmaceutical companies operate manufacturing facilities in Singapore. The country hosts more than sixty biopharmaceutical manufacturing plants, and the sector generated over S$18 billion in manufacturing output in 2023.

Tuas Biomedical Park and Biopolis, both developed by JTC, demonstrate how the government has built research and manufacturing ecosystems that individual companies could not establish on their own.

In 2024, Novartis announced a US$256 million investment to expand its biopharmaceutical manufacturing operations in Singapore. The new facility will incorporate digital technologies and automation in addition to therapeutic antibody production.

Another example is the aircraft MRO industry.

Singapore is home to major MRO operations run by ST Engineering, GE Aerospace, Rolls-Royce, Pratt & Whitney, and Singapore Aero Engine Services Limited (SAESL), covering aircraft, components, and aircraft engines.

Singapore accounts for approximately 10% of the global aircraft MRO market and around 20% of the global aircraft engine MRO market. Put differently, roughly one out of every ten commercial aircraft and one out of every five aircraft engines worldwide is supported by maintenance infrastructure connected to Singapore.

GE Aerospace has invested US$300 million to expand its MRO capabilities in Singapore, establishing a new repair facility in Seletar dedicated to CFM LEAP engines.

ST Engineering has also announced plans to expand its engine MRO facilities at Paya Lebar, increasing annual overhaul capacity to more than 300 engines by 2027.

Semiconductors.

Pharmaceuticals.

Aircraft MRO.

At first glance, these appear to be completely unrelated industries.

However, they share several fundamental characteristics.

They require enormous capital investment.

Developing highly skilled talent takes many years.

Strict quality control and international certification are essential.

They depend on supporting infrastructure such as water, electricity, logistics, research institutions, and regulatory systems.

Once these industrial clusters are established, they cannot easily be relocated to another country.

In 2021, the Singapore government launched **Manufacturing 2030**, setting a goal of increasing manufacturing value-added by 50% by 2030 compared with 2020 levels.

Since then, it has continued updating its Industry Transformation Maps across sectors including electronics, aerospace, energy and chemicals, precision engineering, and logistics, while simultaneously promoting research and development, digitalization, sustainability, and workforce development.

Looking at these policies again, I realized that Singapore is not simply trying to expand its manufacturing sector.

Instead, it is attracting companies with highly specialized capabilities and accumulating functions that are difficult to replace within global value chains.

I had long understood, at least conceptually, that Singapore seeks not to become the largest country, but an indispensable one.

This was not a new discovery.

However, after working inside a semiconductor manufacturing site and revisiting the pharmaceutical and aircraft MRO industries, I finally connected what I had previously viewed as separate industries over the past twenty years into a single strategic structure.

Singapore has not simply chosen growth industries.

It has strategically developed industries that are essential to the global economy but cannot easily be expanded because of the constraints imposed by technology, certification, facilities, skilled labor, and infrastructure.

The semiconductor industry in the AI era is a continuation of this strategy.

Singapore is concentrating bottleneck industries that are becoming increasingly critical to AI growth, including semiconductors, manufacturing equipment, advanced materials, advanced manufacturing, water, electricity, and logistics.

Although I have been connected with Singapore for more than twenty years, only recently have I begun to see semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, and aircraft MRO not as separate industries, but as components of the same national design.

This Research Log records how two decades of observation finally converged into a single understanding of Singapore's national strategy.
