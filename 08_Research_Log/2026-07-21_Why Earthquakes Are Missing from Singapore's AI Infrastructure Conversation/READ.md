# Why Earthquakes Are Missing from Singapore's AI Infrastructure Conversation

In April 2016, the Kumamoto Earthquake struck Japan. My family home was severely damaged.

At the time, I had been living in Singapore for only one year.

My elderly parents evacuated to a shelter, but I was unable to return to Japan. Flights were suspended, and all I could do was watch the situation unfold from Singapore.

I still remember seriously asking myself,

**"Should I really continue living in Singapore?"**

It was only my first year after moving abroad.

At that time, I never imagined that this experience would eventually shape the way I view AI infrastructure ten years later.

## Earthquakes Were Simply Part of the Assumptions in Japan

Before moving to Singapore, I had many opportunities to meet companies that supplied seismic isolation systems for data centers. Some of these systems were designed to protect entire server racks from earthquakes.

Especially after the Great East Japan Earthquake, seismic isolation became an increasingly important topic throughout Japan's data center industry.

In a country where earthquakes are part of everyday reality, discussions about **how to protect buildings** are completely natural.

Living in Japan, I assumed that this was a universal concern.

## In Singapore, Nobody Talks About Seismic Isolation

Today, I work at an FC-BGA (semiconductor substrate) manufacturing site in Singapore.

Every day I observe the equipment and logistics that support the production of semiconductor substrates used for AI servers.

One thing surprised me.

Nobody talks about earthquakes.

Nobody talks about seismic isolation.

Nobody discusses earthquake-resistant equipment.

Although buildings in Singapore are occasionally designed with distant regional earthquakes in mind (for example, tremors from the Aceh earthquake were felt slightly in Singapore), industrial equipment is generally not discussed under the assumption that earthquakes will occur.

This is not because earthquakes are being ignored.

It is simply because Singapore's natural environment is fundamentally different from Japan's.

## AI Infrastructure Is Not Universal

When people discuss AI, they usually focus on GPUs, semiconductors, or data centers.

However, AI infrastructure is actually built upon each country's natural environment and social conditions.

In Japan, important considerations include:

- Earthquakes
- Earthquake-resistant design
- Seismic isolation systems

In Singapore, the priorities are different:

- Stable ground
- Hot and humid climate
- Power supply
- Cooling

These factors shape infrastructure design in entirely different ways.

Even when building AI infrastructure, the underlying assumptions are never the same.

## What I Could Not See Ten Years Ago

During the Kumamoto Earthquake, my only concern was my family.

At that time, the event had nothing to do with AI or semiconductors. TSMC had not yet entered Kumamoto.

Ten years later, after working inside an AI semiconductor manufacturing environment and observing AI infrastructure firsthand, I finally realized something.

**AI infrastructure is never independent of the natural environment.**

The assumption of earthquakes, which was completely natural in Japan, is rarely discussed in Singapore.

Instead, Singapore has its own environmental assumptions.

Experiencing this contrast firsthand changed the way I think about AI infrastructure.

I no longer see it simply as computers or technology.

I now see it as part of a nation's infrastructure philosophy.

## Learning Note

At the time, I never imagined that the Kumamoto Earthquake would one day become connected to AI infrastructure.

However, I believe that understanding does not emerge all at once.

It evolves gradually over time.

The earthquake ten years ago, the seismic isolation technologies I encountered in Japan, and my current work inside Singapore's AI semiconductor industry are not isolated experiences.

They are part of one continuous evolution in how I understand infrastructure.

That is why I believe that understanding AI infrastructure requires observing not only GPUs and semiconductors, but also the natural environment and societal assumptions on which each country's infrastructure is built.
