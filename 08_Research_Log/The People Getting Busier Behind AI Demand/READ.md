# The People Getting Busier Behind AI Demand

When people talk about AI infrastructure, most discussions focus on GPUs, HBM, or optical networking.

Until recently, I was also watching several videos explaining optical networking as the next bottleneck for AI data centers.

The companies frequently mentioned were Broadcom, Marvell, Lumentum, and Coherent.

However, spending time close to a manufacturing environment gradually led me to notice a different group of companies.

Recently, I found myself paying closer attention to fields such as machine vision, optical inspection, precision metrology, and manufacturing equipment.

For example, **Pixel Inc.** of South Korea specializes in machine vision and image processing technologies.

Another Korean company, **Gigavis**, is well known for its Automated Optical Inspection (AOI) systems for PCBs and semiconductor packaging.

In Japan, **Nikon** offers image-based metrology systems such as the NEXIV series, which are widely used for precision measurement in electronics and semiconductor manufacturing.

Meanwhile, **Nidec** is widely recognized as a motor manufacturer, but its business also extends into industrial machinery and manufacturing equipment.

This does not imply that these companies are involved in the same factory or the same manufacturing process.

However, when considering the production of AI-oriented semiconductors and FC-BGA substrates, technologies for image recognition, defect inspection, dimensional measurement, and manufacturing equipment all become essential parts of the ecosystem.

Until now, I had mainly observed AI infrastructure through technologies such as conveyor systems, AMRs, and PLCs.

This time, however, my perspective changed slightly.

Manufacturing is supported by far more than material handling.

The technologies that inspect products, measure tolerances, and maintain manufacturing quality are also fundamental components of AI infrastructure.

---

Of course, I do not know which customers, products, or projects these companies are involved in.

Nor is this article intended to discuss the order status or equipment deployment of any particular company.

Instead, what interests me is the broader structure of how AI demand spreads across surrounding industries.

Technologies that inspect products.

Technologies that measure dimensions.

Technologies that optimize image inspection.

Technologies that maintain manufacturing precision.

As AI semiconductors become more advanced and manufacturing complexity increases, the importance of these technologies is likely to grow as well.

Before working in this manufacturing environment, I tended to think of AI infrastructure simply as GPUs and semiconductors.

In reality, however, semiconductor manufacturing consists of countless supporting processes, including material handling, automation, optical inspection, precision metrology, quality assurance, and production ramp-up.

Only after these processes work together can a single FC-BGA substrate become a finished product.

AI is not advancing through software alone.

Nor is it advancing solely through semiconductor design.

Behind the scenes, companies and engineers specializing in measurement and inspection technologies are also becoming part of AI infrastructure.

This observation gave me a new perspective on AI demand—one viewed through the lens of the inspection and metrology industry.

---

# Learning Note

## Pixel Inc.

A South Korean company specializing in machine vision and image processing technologies. It develops image inspection and image processing systems for semiconductor and PCB manufacturing. As AI demand continues to grow, high-precision inspection technologies are likely to become increasingly important.

## Gigavis

A South Korean manufacturer of Automated Optical Inspection (AOI) systems. The company develops inspection equipment for FC-BGA substrates and PCBs, helping detect microscopic defects in advanced semiconductor packaging.

## Nikon

Nikon's NEXIV series is widely used as an image-based metrology system in electronics and semiconductor manufacturing. As semiconductor structures continue to shrink, precision dimensional measurement becomes increasingly critical.

## Nidec

Beyond its well-known motor business, Nidec has expanded into semiconductor manufacturing equipment, industrial machinery, and precision engineering. Based on my observations, I felt that activity on the manufacturing equipment side may also be increasing, although I have not confirmed the specific nature of that work.

# What I Learned Today

- AI demand may extend beyond GPU manufacturers to inspection, metrology, and manufacturing equipment companies.
- In manufacturing environments, changes may first become visible through the movement of people before they become apparent through equipment or public announcements.
- Continuous observation of manufacturing sites can reveal structural changes that are difficult to recognize through corporate news alone.
