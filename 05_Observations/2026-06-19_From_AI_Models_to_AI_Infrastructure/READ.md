# From AI Models to AI Infrastructure

## Observation

For a long time, I viewed AI primarily through the lens of model competition.

OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, DeepSeek.

Which company would build the most capable model?

Which model would achieve the highest performance?

Which organization would move closest to AGI?

Much of the public discussion around AI is framed in these terms.

I was no exception.

I assumed that the evolution of AI was fundamentally the evolution of models, and that competition would be determined primarily by advances in model development.

---

## Entering the Factory

My perspective began to change after entering an FC-BGA substrate manufacturing environment.

For the first time, I had the opportunity to observe the full production ecosystem behind a critical component of modern computing infrastructure.

Cleanrooms.

Automated material handling systems.

Manufacturing equipment.

Inspection processes.

And production systems designed to operate continuously around the clock.

Very little discussion inside the factory focused on AI models themselves.

Instead, what I encountered was the enormous manufacturing capability and capital investment required to support the broader AI industry.

---

## A Different View of Competition

What I observed inside the factory was a very different form of competition.

AI models require semiconductors.

Semiconductors require manufacturing equipment.

Manufacturing equipment requires factories.

Factories require power, water, logistics systems, maintenance capabilities, and skilled personnel.

In other words, AI models do not exist independently.

They are built upon a vast industrial system.

Previously, I viewed AI competition primarily as competition between models.

After observing the factory environment, I began to see it as competition between entire industrial ecosystems.

---

## The Infrastructure Behind AI

What most users see is the AI model.

They interact with ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini.

But behind those systems lies a much larger infrastructure.

FC-BGA substrates.

Semiconductor manufacturing equipment.

Data centers.

Power infrastructure.

Automated transport systems.

Global supply chains.

In recent years, growing attention has been paid to data centers and power consumption as AI infrastructure expands.

I also understood AI infrastructure largely through that perspective.

However, observing FC-BGA manufacturing firsthand made me increasingly aware of the layers that exist even further upstream.

The manufacturing equipment.

The production facilities.

The supply chains that enable semiconductor production itself.

An AI model cannot exist without any of these components.

The factory floor made that reality impossible to ignore.

---

## Reflection

My experience inside an FC-BGA manufacturing environment significantly changed the way I think about AI.

Viewing AI as software is not wrong.

But it is incomplete.

AI is both a software phenomenon and an infrastructure phenomenon.

Future competition will not be determined solely by model performance.

Semiconductors.

Manufacturing capacity.

Capital investment.

Power infrastructure.

Data centers.

Supply chains.

All of these form part of the foundation upon which AI systems are built.

By observing the full manufacturing process inside an FC-BGA factory, I gradually shifted from viewing AI as a competition between models to viewing it as a competition between infrastructures.

That shift in perspective ultimately influenced the way I think about AI Capitalism and the broader themes explored in AI Civilization Atlas.

To understand AI, it is no longer sufficient to look only at the models.

We must also understand the infrastructure that makes those models possible.
