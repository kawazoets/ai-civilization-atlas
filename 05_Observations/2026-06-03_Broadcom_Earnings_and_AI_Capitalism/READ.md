# From Broadcom Earnings to AI Capitalism

**Observation Date:** 2026-06-19

## Observation

Broadcom reported another strong quarter.

### Earnings Snapshot

Broadcom's fiscal Q2 2026 results were exceptionally strong.

* Adjusted revenue: US$22.19 billion (+48% YoY)
* Net income: US$9.31 billion (+88% YoY)
* AI semiconductor revenue: US$10.8 billion (2.4x YoY)

On the surface, these numbers suggest a company operating at full strength.

Yet despite these results, the stock price declined following the earnings announcement.

This contrast is what makes the observation interesting.

Revenue, profitability, and AI-related demand remained robust. The company continues to benefit from the expansion of AI infrastructure, particularly in networking, custom AI accelerators, and data center-related investments.

Despite these strong financial results, the stock price declined following the earnings announcement.

From a traditional financial perspective, this appears contradictory. Strong earnings are generally expected to support higher valuations. However, market reactions increasingly suggest that financial performance alone is no longer sufficient to explain price movements.

The market is not merely evaluating what a company has achieved. It is evaluating what it expects the company to become.

## A Different Observation from the Factory Floor

At the same time, I am currently working inside an FC-BGA substrate manufacturing environment that supplies infrastructure connected to the AI ecosystem.

In recent months, discussions regarding a potential second factory expansion have become increasingly visible.

While no official announcement has been made, the existence of such discussions is itself noteworthy.

Factory expansion decisions are not made based on short-term market sentiment. They are based on long-term expectations regarding demand, capacity utilization, customer commitments, and infrastructure requirements.

This creates an interesting contrast.

Financial markets reacted negatively to Broadcom's earnings announcement.

Yet observations from the manufacturing side suggest that the broader AI infrastructure buildout continues.

## The Limits of Financial Observation

For many years, I viewed companies primarily through financial statements, earnings reports, and market data.

However, exposure to semiconductor manufacturing has gradually changed that perspective.

Financial statements are historical records.

Factory construction is a statement about the future.

An earnings report describes what happened.

A new production facility reflects what companies believe will happen.

When viewed through this lens, the market and the factory are observing different timelines.

One is reacting to expectations.

The other is preparing for future demand.

## Connection to AI Capitalism

This observation connects directly to a central theme of AI Capitalism.

In traditional capitalism, financial performance served as the primary signal for capital allocation.

In AI Capitalism, evaluation increasingly moves ahead of realized performance.

Capital is allocated not only according to results, but according to anticipated strategic relevance within AI-driven infrastructure networks.

This helps explain why strong earnings may not automatically lead to higher valuations.

The market is evaluating future positioning rather than current profitability alone.

The question is no longer:

*"How much profit did the company generate?"*

The question increasingly becomes:

*"How important will this company be within future AI infrastructure?"*


## A Broader Infrastructure Signal

What makes this observation particularly interesting is that the potential expansion of the facility is not occurring in isolation.

In recent months, Singapore has also seen continued investment across semiconductor and AI-related infrastructure, including new manufacturing and technology facilities.

Viewed individually, these projects may appear unrelated.

Viewed collectively, they suggest a broader commitment to positioning Singapore within the global AI infrastructure supply chain.

From this perspective, the rumored the expansion is not merely a factory story.

It may be one small component within a much larger infrastructure buildout.


## Reflection

The most important insight from observing Broadcom is not whether the stock price rises or falls after earnings.

The more important observation is the growing gap between financial observation and infrastructure observation.

A financial analyst may see a quarterly report.

A factory operator may see future capacity requirements.

An investor may see valuation.

An engineer may see production constraints.

These observations are not necessarily contradictory.

They are simply observing different layers of the same system.

This realization is one of the reasons my thinking gradually evolved toward AI Capitalism.

The further I moved from software and financial reporting alone, and the closer I moved toward semiconductor manufacturing, infrastructure deployment, and industrial systems, the more difficult it became to explain the AI economy using traditional financial frameworks alone.
