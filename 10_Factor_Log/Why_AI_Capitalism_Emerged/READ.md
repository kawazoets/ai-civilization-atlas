# Why AI Capitalism Emerged

## Introduction

I completed the manuscript for *AI Capitalism*. At the time, I believed the theory was largely complete.

Reality, however, proved otherwise.

After a period of unemployment, I accepted a short-term contract position with less-than-ideal conditions. The job happened to be at an FC-BGA substrate manufacturing facility.

For the first time, I found myself observing the physical production environment behind AI.

Looking back, however, AI Capitalism had begun long before that experience.

The concept emerged while I was writing papers on AI-Scored Society and AIO. It was not a sudden idea. Rather, it was the result of a series of observations that gradually converged into a single framework.

---

# Factor 1

## Corporate Value Cannot Be Explained by Profit Alone

For years, I had been examining corporate financial statements and had grown increasingly uncomfortable with the relationship between profit and corporate valuation.

Some profitable companies received little attention from the market.

Others continued to receive high valuations despite operating at a loss.

Accounting and finance could explain part of this phenomenon, but over time I felt that these explanations were becoming increasingly insufficient.

Alphabet's earnings reports were particularly confusing to me.

The company was aggressively investing in AI development and introducing AI Overviews, yet much of its valuation continued to be justified through search advertising revenue.

The contradiction raised several questions.

What exactly was the market evaluating?

Why could profit alone no longer explain corporate value?

These questions remained with me for a long time.

---

# Factor 2

## Observing Search and Visibility

Another major factor was my long-standing interest in search and visibility.

For many years, I observed changes in search engines and SEO.

Gradually, I came to recognize a reality that many users rarely notice.

People appear to choose information freely, but in practice, visibility is determined by systems before human choice takes place.

What becomes visible?

What remains invisible?

AI-driven selection occurs first.

Human decision-making comes afterward.

At the same time, I witnessed dramatic declines in traffic across affiliate businesses, content aggregation sites, and search-dependent media.

These observations naturally led to the framework that later became AI-Scored Society.

---

# Factor 3

## The Emergence of AI-Scored Society

Even before generative AI became mainstream, I was interested in the growing role of AI in evaluation.

AI does not merely generate answers.

It determines rankings.

It determines recommendations.

It determines visibility.

It influences trust.

It allocates responsibility.

I began to see AI as a mechanism for distributing social evaluation.

This eventually led me to formulate the concept of AI-Scored Society.

However, this framework generated a new question.

If AI was changing the allocation of social evaluation, was it also changing the allocation of capital?

---

# Factor 4

## Connecting Evaluation and Capital

This became the direct starting point of AI Capitalism.

As I continued observing financial markets, it became increasingly difficult to believe that capital moved solely in response to profit.

This was particularly visible in the earnings reports of AI hyperscalers.

Markets appeared to respond not only to financial performance but also to expectations and evaluation signals.

More importantly, AI itself seemed to be playing an increasing role in shaping those signals.

Evaluation and capital were not separate phenomena.

Evaluation attracted capital.

Capital reinforced evaluation.

As this feedback loop became visible, I began to suspect that a structure different from traditional capitalism was emerging.

That realization became the foundation of AI Capitalism.

---

# Factor 5

## What Changed After the Paper Was Written

After completing AI Capitalism, another event significantly expanded my understanding of AI.

I began working inside a Broadcom-related FC-BGA substrate manufacturing facility.

For the first time, I was able to observe the actual production process behind advanced semiconductor substrates.

Until then, my primary interest had been focused on AI evaluation systems and capital allocation.

The manufacturing environment introduced an entirely different perspective.

To understand AI, discussing GPU performance alone was no longer sufficient.

AI does not exist solely inside cloud infrastructure.

It requires semiconductors.

Semiconductors require manufacturing equipment.

Observing substrate manufacturing allowed me to see the physical foundation of AI for the first time.

---

# Factor 6

## Broadcom, Data Centers, and Electricity

As I continued observing FC-BGA production, I became increasingly interested in Broadcom.

Following Broadcom eventually led me to data centers.

Following data centers eventually led me to electricity.

At that point, I arrived at a simple realization:

* AI cannot exist without electricity.
* AI models require computational power.
* Computational power requires semiconductors.
* Semiconductors require manufacturing equipment.
* All of these depend on electricity.

Today, I no longer see AI merely as software or models.

Instead, I see AI as a vast industrial system supported by electricity, semiconductors, manufacturing infrastructure, and capital.

---

AI Capitalism did not emerge suddenly.

It emerged from a series of observations:

* discomfort with conventional explanations of corporate value,
* observations of search and visibility,
* interest in AI-driven evaluation systems,
* and the connection between evaluation and capital.

The framework was the product of accumulated observations rather than a single insight.

Even today, my understanding of AI continues to evolve through direct observation of manufacturing environments.
